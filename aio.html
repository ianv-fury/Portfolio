AI Overviews

After a successful foray into AI-powered search with Search to Prompt, we set our sights on trying something more ambitious. The Search team has long had reason to believe that users want to do more with their queries than simple retrieval. Nearly 30% of searches are what we consider "broad intents" – queries where the user is expressing that they're looking to explore a genre, mood or topic without a clear goal in mind. And as users began to adapt to AI-powered search, we noticed that queries were getting longer and more complex.

As a product organization, we wanted to be thoughtful about how we applied AI. Rather than recreating Google Search inside Spotify, we wanted to focus on our superpower: connecting people to content. All of our product decisions stemmed from the principle that we let content lead the way, and use AI as a tool to contextualize recommendations and tell stories about that content.

Ideation

I began this process by working with my product partners to shape user stories around this feature. We settled on three stories we would support for MVP, and two that we believed in, but wouldn't support yet.

✓

Ask about a creator / content

"Who is Jack Antonoff?"

✓

Discover new music

"What's trending in hip hop right now?"

✓

Understand your listening

"Who was my top artist last month?"

✗

Explore a non-music topic

"What's going on in the news right now?"

✗

Take an action / change a setting

"Help me manage my subscription"

Next, I performed a competitive audit of apps that have added similar features, and translated common patterns into wireframes. I focused on creating content hierarchies that made sense for Spotify's content needs, and the user stories that we were supporting.

Testing & Iteration

We performed 3 rounds of mixed-methods research that included research-led foundational interviews and participatory design sessions, followed by designer-led usability testing and employee dog-fooding.

Key findings

Despite a growing trend in longer searches, many users didn't expect this to be possible at Spotify. Deeper education was needed.

Users expressed excitement when Spotify did things beyond what they could "google for themselves".

Users gravitated towards longer answers than we expected, and liked the ability to dive deeper.

Feature education

In order to raise user awareness, we decided to place prompt-style suggestions earlier in the flow. At first, we experimented with alternate icons, highlighting and motion. Ultimately, based on resource constraints and a long-standing internal debate about AI branding, we decided to let the prompt suggestions speak for themselves.

Highlighting Spotify's unique capabilities

One clear place where we could add further value was in session generation. When users ask us a question, there's usually an underlying content request baked in. AI Overviews can anticipate the listening need as well as the informational need, and call our Search to Prompt service to create a session that lets the user dive right in.

Playing with answer length

Our initial thinking was that for a feature like this, less is more. Our early overviews were straight to the point and attempted to cut everything but a direct answer. This did not survive contact with the user. In the participatory design session, 8 out of 10 users picked out longer answers, and articulated that the longer answers felt "more complete" and "more interesting".

We settled on two paragraphs, with an optional 3rd paragraph if needed. The first paragraph would always be visible above the fold, and the rest would be hidden behind a "see more" so as to not overwhelm the user with text, and make sure content was always visible above the fold.

Prompting

With limited content design resources, I also served as the key prompt designer on the product. Working with a senior ML engineer and our AI content tone guide, I built a prompt that:

  • Sounded like Spotify's voice (friendly, not jargony, not "music insider")
  • Output clean YAML that the client could reliably parse
  • Hit every insight from user research

To pressure-test the prompt before the client build was ready, my engineering partner built an internal tool that let Spotify employees try the service in a web environment. I ran several dog-fooding workshops where coworkers tested specific categories of interest, plus anything else they wanted to throw at it. This surfaced opportunities we couldn't have caught in formal usability testing.

Prompt writing

One issue that we faced early on was limited Content design resourcing. Our AI-focused content designer was stretched thin across too many products, so under his supervision I took the lead on crafting the core prompt.

Finally putting my English minor to work, I partnered with a machine learning engineer to write and optimize a prompt to power the Search Overviews experience.

Prompt testing

Employee feedback allowed us to continually improve the prompt before the client build was ready to test. Ultimately, we created a loop where feedback was distilled into key takeaways, and those takeaways were fed into the prompt for compounding improvements.

What's next

Prompted results pages

Today, AI Overviews only affect the top portion of the page. This often leaves the "More Results" options below the fold feeling useless. We can do better. Currently working on designs for:

  • Groups with dynamic titles and subheads
  • Content groupings that let the user explore their question from different angles

Fulfilling all intents

Due to resource constraints, we focused heavily in making the product work well for music first.

Realistically, users can and will try to ask for anything. Next, I want to tackle:

  • First class podcast and audiobook searches
  • Helping users redirect to content when they ask about something unrelated to content

Expand query coverage

Right now, AI Overviews is only serving "Ask" intents.

As latency improves, we can also start to serve genre, song, and artist queries with more compelling content. We'd try implementing AI overviews further down the page, so as to not interfere with users hitting play when trying to retrieve content.
